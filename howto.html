How to Crochet for Beginners

What is Crochet?
Crochet is a craft technique that involves using a hooked needle to create fabric by interlocking loops of yarn or thread. It is similar to knitting, but instead of using two needles, crochet uses a single hook. From blankets to hats to fancy lace, there's no shortage of what you can make. The best part? You've got endless possibilities! We've put together a simple, step-by-step guide to help you kick off your crochet journey.

What do I need to begin crocheting?
Luckily, you don't need much to dive into crochet – just two things:

  • Yarn
  • Crochet Hook

Picking Your Yarn
Choosing the right yarn sets the stage for how your project turns out. Yarn comes in all weights, colours, and materials. Beginners might find it easier to start with medium-weight yarn in a light colour. This makes it simpler to see your stitches and fix any slip-ups as you go.

Choosing Your Hook
Crochet hooks come in different sizes and materials like metal, plastic, bamboo, and wood. The size of your hook depends on your yarn's weight and what size you're aiming for. If you're unsure, check the yarn label – it usually tells you the recommended hook size.

Now that you have your materials - let’s crochet! Here you’ll find a handy step by step guide that you can refer back to at any time. Just remember, learning takes time, and it's okay to make mistakes along the way.

A Step-by-Step Guide to Crochet

How to Hold Your Crochet Hook
There are two main ways to hold your hook: the pencil grip and the knife grip. Choose whichever feels most comfortable for you.

Method 1 - The Pencil Grip
Hold the hook like you would a pencil – thumb on the flat part, hook pointing down, and the rest of your fingers curled around it.

Method 2 - The Knife Grip
Place your right hand over the hook and grasp it between your thumb and index finger. Your left hand controls the yarn and holds the stitches.

Experiment to find what works best for you. The goal is to have a grip that lets you handle the hook smoothly and stay in control while you work.

How to Hold Yarn When Crocheting
Getting the hang of holding your yarn is crucial for keeping your tension consistent. Tension is important in crochet because it affects the overall appearance and size of your project. When you crochet with consistent tension, it ensures that your stitches are uniform in size and appearance. This is especially important when working on projects that require precise measurements. This affects how your stitches look and how your project turns out. Hold your non-dominant hand out in front of you, face down. Bring the initial strand of yarn under your little finger, ring finger, or middle finger. Then wrap it over your index finger. Control the tension by squeezing your fingers together as you work a stitch and pull more yarn into your project. When you squeeze, you'll add more tension and your stitches will be smaller. Less squeezing results in a lower tension and more loose stitching.

How to Make a Slip Knot
First things first, you need a slip knot to start. Here's how:

  1. Make a loop with your yarn, leaving a tail for later.
  2. Bring the tail over the loop and insert it underneath.
  3. Pull the tail to tighten the knot.

Making a Chain Stitch
Now, let's make a starting chain, which is the foundation for your project:

  1. Hold the short tail of the slip knot in your left hand and the hook in your right.
  2. Yarn over (bring the yarn over the hook from back to front) and pull it through the loop on your hook. This creates a new loop on the hook, which is your first chain stitch.
  3. Keep making chain stitches until you've got the number you need.

Consistency is key here – practice makes perfect!

Counting the Chains
When you count chains, don't include the slip knot or the loop on your hook. Work your first stitch between the Vs of each chain stitch.

The first row can be tough, especially for newbies. Here are some tips to help you out:

  • Take your time and be patient with yourself.
  • Try using a slightly larger hook to make it easier.
  • Keep your yarn tension just right.
  • Practise with a foundation chain: before starting your actual project, practise making a foundation chain on its own. This will give you a chance to get comfortable with creating chain stitches and help you figure out where to insert your hook.
  • Use stitch markers to keep track of your rows. This will help you keep track of where your rows begin and prevent any confusion.
  • Always count your stitches as you go to avoid any mistakes.
  • Use a stable surface: If you find it hard to hold the fabric while crocheting into the chain, place your work on a stable surface like a table or lap desk to provide support.

All About Stitches

Single Crochet (SC)
The single crochet is a basic stitch that's great for beginners. Once you've got your foundation chain, you can start working single crochet stitches into it:

  1. Insert your hook into the desired stitch. The first stitch is usually the second chain from the hook, but it can vary depending on the pattern.
  2. Yarn over: Once your hook is inserted, wrap the yarn over the hook from back to front, moving in an anticlockwise direction.
  3. Pull through: draw the wrapped yarn through the stitch. Now, you should have two loops on your hook.
  4. Yarn over and pull through both loops: wrap the yarn over the hook again and pull it through both loops on the hook. This completes one single crochet stitch.
  5. Continue across the row: Repeat steps 1 to 4 for each stitch in the foundation chain until you reach the end of the row.
  6. Turning chain: When you reach the end of the row, you'll need to turn your work to start the next row. To do this, chain one (or as many as required by the pattern) and turn your work.

Double Crochet (DC)
The double crochet is a bit taller than the single and creates a more open and airy fabric.

  1. Yarn over: wrap the yarn from back to front around your hook, moving in an anticlockwise direction.
  2. Insert your hook into the desired stitch. The first stitch is usually the second chain from the hook, but it can vary depending on the pattern.
  3. Yarn over and pull through: yarn over again and pull it through the stitch you inserted your hook into. Now, you should have three loops on your hook.
  4. Yarn over and pull through two loops: yarn over once more and pull it through the first two loops on your hook. You should have two loops remaining on the hook.
  5. Yarn over and pull through the last two loops: yarn over one last time and pull it through the remaining two loops on your hook. This completes one double crochet stitch.
  6. Continue across the row: Repeat steps 1 to 5 for each stitch in the foundation chain until you reach the end of the row.
  7. Turning chain: When you reach the end of the row, you'll need to turn your work to start the next row. For double crochet, typically chain three at the end of the row and turn your work.

Tips:

  • Keep your tension steady for even stitches.
  • Count your stitches at the end of each row to ensure you haven't missed or added any stitches
  • The last stitch of each row can be a bit tricky to identify. Pay attention to the top of the turning chain from the previous row, and make sure to work into it.

Finishing Touches
When you've finished your project, it's time to wrap things up:

Wrap up your project:

  • Fasten off: when you're done, cut the yarn, leaving a tail of about 6 inches (15 cm) or more. Then, loop the tail through the last stitch on your hook to make a secure knot.
  • Weave in ends: after fastening off, you'll have yarn tails at both ends of your work. Use a yarn needle or crochet hook to weave these tails into the stitches on the backside of your project. This keeps them from unravelling.
  • Block your project (optional): blocking helps shape and size your crochet piece. Depending on the yarn and project, you might want to steam block, wet block, or use special tools for blocking.
  • Add embellishments (optional): if you want to jazz up your project with buttons, zips, or other extras, now's the time to do it.
  • Finish off decorative edges (optional): for projects like blankets or shawls with fancy edges, consider adding a border for a polished look. Just pick your favourite pattern and follow the instructions.
  • Wash and block (optional): if needed, wash your project following the yarn's care instructions, then block it to get the perfect shape and size.
  • Enjoy your finished project: once you've dotted all the i's and crossed all the t's, your crochet masterpiece is ready to show off or give as a gift!

Crochet might seem tricky at first, but with practice and a bit of patience, you'll soon be whipping up all sorts of wonderful creations. Happy hooking!
